Information Design is playing an increasingly critical role in everyday journalism. The movement from word and picture to “words within diagrams” is building a new form of truth-telling and storytelling — and with it, a new journalistic aesthetic.
–Giorgia Lupi

The Architecture of a Data Visualization; Feb 26, 2015

Lupi is certainly not holding anything back in this quote, or in the mission statement that this quote serves as introduction. Her tokens of brilliance are densely packed. Let's unravel some of them.

Lupi distinguishes information design as a seperate media form from pictures. "Words within diagrams" is incredibly apt definition to expalin that distinction. Information designs are meant to be read, rather than looked at. Images often serve as accent pieces, receiving a glance as the reader turns the page or finishes a paragraph. Informaiton designs are focal points. They entice and demand concentration.

Under that description of Information Design, the lead image is just than, an image. It hardly demands attention, and does not ask the reader to delve deeper. It teasingly asks the viewer to look for something interesting, without providing the data or the means to do so. However, it does this honestly or at least the words that go along with it make it honest. Other information designs can be a lot less honest.

It begs the question of honesty and communication pitfalls in this "new jouranlistic aesthetic". Information design can assuredly unlock thoughts that we could not previously think; however, that does not make those thoughts true. Pessimism aside, there are certainly steps forward from here. The "democritzation" of a media form like Information Design will certainly have its kinks to work out; best practices (e.g. Tufte-ian) will take time to propogate through a burgeoning, new community. Readers and the concerned public will adjust as well. The need for graphical literacy is going beyond the classroom the scientist's lab. It is quickly becoming a foundational tool to us to understand and interpret the world.

Pandey et. al., "How Deceptive are Deceptive Visualizations?: An Empirical Analysis of Common Distortion Techniques", in CHI 2015
